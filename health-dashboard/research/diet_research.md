# Diet Research Memo — Phase 5a (Cardio Tracking Project)

**Purpose:** Evidence review of four dietary patterns (DASH, Mediterranean, Blue Zones, Portfolio) for an endurance athlete (cyclist/runner) whose LDL cholesterol is pharmacologically managed. Diet's primary job here is blood pressure reduction and modest weight loss, not LDL-lowering. This document is research background only, not medical advice, and contains no personal health data, targets, or treatment specifics — just publicly published trial results and dietary-guideline numbers.

**Scope note on evidence quality:** DASH and Mediterranean rest on randomized controlled trial (RCT) evidence with hard cardiovascular endpoints (PREDIMED) or blood-pressure endpoints (DASH, DASH-Sodium). Portfolio diet evidence is a mix of short RCTs and a pooled meta-analysis, focused on LDL and other lipid/cardiometabolic markers rather than hard clinical endpoints. Blue Zones evidence is almost entirely observational/epidemiological (cohort studies, ecological/longevity claims), with real controversy in the peer-reviewed literature about the reliability of the underlying longevity data — this is flagged explicitly below and should be weighted accordingly.

## 1. DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)

### Evidence with effect sizes

- **Original DASH trial** (Appel et al., 1997, *NEJM*, "A Clinical Trial of the Effects of Dietary Patterns on Blood Pressure"): an 8-week, multicenter, controlled feeding trial. Among participants with hypertension, the DASH diet lowered systolic blood pressure by **11.4 mmHg** and diastolic blood pressure by **5.5 mmHg** more than the control (typical American) diet. Across the full study cohort the DASH diet reduction was smaller in absolute terms but still significant, and NHLBI notes the magnitude was comparable to what single-drug antihypertensive therapy achieves. [NEJM 1997](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199704173361601) — [2 Minute Medicine summary](https://www.2minutemedicine.com/the-dash-trial-diet-change-significantly-reduces-blood-pressure-classics-series/)
- **DASH-Sodium trial** (Sacks et al., 2001, *NEJM*): a 412-participant, 3-arm sodium-level × diet-pattern feeding trial. Versus the higher-sodium control diet, the DASH diet reduced systolic BP by **5.9, 7.2, and 8.9 mmHg** at the higher, intermediate, and lower sodium levels respectively, and diastolic BP by **2.9, 3.5, and 4.5 mmHg** at those same levels. Reducing sodium *within* the control diet alone (no DASH food pattern) cut systolic BP by **2.1–6.7 mmHg** depending on the sodium step-down. Combining the DASH diet with the lowest (1,500 mg) sodium level produced the largest blood-pressure benefit of any arm. [NEJM 2001](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM200101043440101) — [NHLBI DASH research summary](https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/education/dash/research)
- Follow-up analyses of the DASH trial data also reported **LDL cholesterol reductions**, though this was a secondary finding, not the trial's primary endpoint. [NHLBI DASH research summary](https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/education/dash/research)
- In the American Heart Association's own 2023 comparative scoring of popular diets against its dietary guidance, **DASH scored 100/100**, the only diet in the comparison to do so. [AHA: How 10 popular diets scored for heart health](https://www.heart.org/en/news/2023/04/27/heres-how-10-popular-diets-scored-for-heart-health)

### Core food rules (practical terms)

- **Eat more:** vegetables, fruits, whole grains, fat-free/low-fat dairy, fish, poultry, beans, nuts, and vegetable oils.
- **Eat less:** foods high in saturated fat (fatty meats, full-fat dairy, tropical oils like coconut/palm), sugar-sweetened beverages, and sweets.
- No special/exotic foods required — it's built from ordinary grocery-store items following daily/weekly serving counts (on a 2,000-kcal reference diet: 6–8 grain servings, 4–5 vegetable, 4–5 fruit, 2–3 low-fat dairy, ≤6 meat/poultry/fish, 2–3 fats/oils, 4–5/week nuts-legumes, ≤5/week sweets).

[NHLBI DASH Eating Plan](https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/dash-eating-plan)

### Sodium / potassium / fiber / saturated-fat targets (DASH's own published guidance)

- **Sodium:** standard DASH ceiling **2,300 mg/day**; a "lower sodium" version targets **1,500 mg/day** for larger BP benefit.
- **Potassium:** target of roughly **4,700 mg/day**, intended to work synergistically with sodium reduction.
- **Saturated fat:** ≤ **6% of total calories**; **total fat** ≤ **27% of total calories**.
- **Fiber:** not given as a single isolated gram target in the sources reviewed here, but the vegetable/fruit/whole-grain/legume serving counts are explicitly designed to push fiber intake well above typical U.S. averages.

[NHLBI DASH Eating Plan](https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/dash-eating-plan) — [NHLBI: Your Guide to Lowering Blood Pressure With DASH (PDF)](https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/files/docs/public/heart/new_dash.pdf)

## 2. Mediterranean Diet

### Evidence with effect sizes

- **Cochrane systematic review** ("Mediterranean-style diet for the primary and secondary prevention of cardiovascular disease," 30 RCTs, 12,461 participants): for primary prevention, moderate-quality evidence showed the Mediterranean diet reduced **systolic BP by 2.99 mmHg** (95% CI −3.45 to −2.53) and **diastolic BP by 2.0 mmHg** (95% CI −2.29 to −1.71) versus minimal/no intervention, and produced a **small LDL-cholesterol reduction of about 0.15 mmol/L** (95% CI −0.27 to −0.02) versus other comparator diets. The review also found moderate-quality evidence of reduced stroke incidence for primary prevention, but rated overall certainty for hard cardiovascular endpoints as still uncertain given the limited number of large trials. [Cochrane Library summary](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD009825_mediterranean-style-diet-prevention-cardiovascular-disease) — [Full review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7427685/)
- **PREDIMED trial** (Estruch et al., corrected/republished 2018, *NEJM*, "Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease with a Mediterranean Diet"): 7,447 high-cardiovascular-risk participants randomized to Mediterranean diet + extra-virgin olive oil, Mediterranean diet + mixed nuts, or a low-fat control diet. The Mediterranean+olive-oil arm showed roughly a **30% relative risk reduction** in the composite endpoint of myocardial infarction, stroke, or cardiovascular death (hazard ratio consistent with the original 2013 report after the 2018 statistical reanalysis following a randomization-protocol correction). The Cochrane review separately reports PREDIMED's stroke-specific hazard ratio at **0.60** (95% CI 0.45–0.80). [NEJM 2018 (corrected)](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1800389) — [Harvard Chan School: PREDIMED retraction & republication explainer](https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/2018/06/22/predimed-retraction-republication/)
- In the AHA's 2023 diet-scoring comparison, **Mediterranean scored 89/100**, just behind DASH, with the score docked mainly for its allowance of moderate alcohol and its lack of an explicit sodium ceiling. [AHA: How 10 popular diets scored for heart health](https://www.heart.org/en/news/2023/04/27/heres-how-10-popular-diets-scored-for-heart-health)

### Core food rules (practical terms)

- **Eat more:** fruits, vegetables, whole grains, potatoes, beans/legumes, nuts and seeds; olive oil as the primary fat source.
- **Moderate:** dairy, eggs, fish, and poultry — fish and poultry preferred over red meat; wine in low-to-moderate amounts with meals (optional).
- **Minimal processing** is a core principle — whole/minimally processed plant foods are the base of the pattern.

[AHA: Mediterranean diet](https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/healthy-eating/eat-smart/nutrition-basics/mediterranean-diet)

### Sodium / potassium / fiber / saturated-fat targets

- The Mediterranean diet, as published, does **not** carry a formal numeric sodium ceiling like DASH — sodium reduction is not its organizing principle.
- It emphasizes replacing saturated fat with **unsaturated fat** (olive oil, nuts, fatty fish) rather than specifying a hard saturated-fat percentage in its own guidance; the AHA's general cardiovascular dietary guidance (used alongside Mediterranean-style eating) calls for keeping saturated fat low and prioritizing unsaturated sources. [AHA: Mediterranean diet](https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/healthy-eating/eat-smart/nutrition-basics/mediterranean-diet)
- Potassium and fiber are increased indirectly through high produce, legume, and whole-grain intake rather than through a stated gram target in the sources reviewed.

## 3. Blue Zones

### Evidence with effect sizes

- Blue Zones is an observational/epidemiological concept (Dan Buettner and collaborators), not a set of RCTs. The **Adventist Health Study-2** (Loma Linda, CA — the one U.S. "Blue Zone," an intensively plant-based Seventh-day Adventist cohort of 96,000+ participants) reports that vegetarians/pescatarians in the cohort have **lower rates of hypertension** and lower LDL cholesterol and triglycerides than non-vegetarians in the same community, alongside longer lifespan — but these are cohort associations, not controlled-trial effect sizes. [Adventist Health Study background](https://www.ingredicheck.app/blog/why-the-adventist-health-study-changed-plant-based-nutrition-science) — [Blue Zone Loma Linda lifestyle study, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11556529/)
- Component-level observational associations cited in the literature: a daily ~28 g serving of nuts is associated with a **21% lower** heart-disease risk, and each daily serving of whole grains is associated with roughly a **7% lower** mortality risk, in general nutrition-epidemiology literature referenced in Blue Zones research summaries (not a controlled trial of the Blue Zones pattern itself). [Blue Zones dietary pattern research overview](https://www.bluezones.com/research/)
- **Important caveat, from the peer-reviewed literature itself:** multiple recent critical reviews directly challenge the evidentiary basis of the "Blue Zones" concept. A 2024 review argues there is an "absence of scientific evidence relating community lifestyle to longevity" in the Blue Zones regions, and a 2025 analysis raises specific data-reliability concerns — e.g., that Loma Linda was added to the Blue Zones list for U.S. representativeness reasons rather than on directly comparable demographic methodology, and that Okinawan centenarian-registry data was reconstructed after WWII-era record destruction, raising questions about its accuracy. [Critical review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12048395/) — [Blue Zone dietary patterns critical review](https://www.foodandnutritionjournal.org/volume13number2/blue-zone-dietary-patterns-telomere-length-maintenance-and-longevity-a-critical-review/)
- Net read: directionally consistent with cardiovascular benefit (heavy legume/plant intake, minimal processed food, high physical activity as part of daily life), but **no RCT-grade quantified BP or LDL effect size exists for "the Blue Zones diet" as a pattern** — treat it as a lower-certainty, complementary layer rather than a primary evidence-based intervention.

### Core food rules (practical terms)

- **"Plant slant":** aim for roughly 95–100% plant-based content on the plate; the guideline is essentially whole, recognizable, minimally processed foods.
- **Beans/legumes daily:** at least a half-cup of cooked beans per day is the single most consistent food across all five regions (black beans in Nicoya, lentils/garbanzo/white beans in the Mediterranean zones, soybeans in Okinawa).
- **Nuts:** roughly two handfuls (~2 oz) daily.
- **Meat:** treated as a rare/celebratory food — roughly 2 oz, about five times a month.
- **Fish:** modest amounts (up to ~3 oz, up to three times weekly), favoring small fish.
- **Dairy/eggs:** minimized; eggs capped around 3/week in the traditional pattern.
- **Added sugar:** kept low (traditional pattern estimate ~28 g/day vs. much higher typical U.S. intake).
- **Eating pattern/timing:** stop eating at ~80% fullness ("hara hachi bu"), and the diets are eaten in a social context rather than alone.

[Blue Zones food guidelines](https://www.bluezones.com/recipes/food-guidelines/) — [Blue Zones Power 9 — Eat Wisely](https://power9.bluezonesproject.com/eat-wisely)

### Sodium / potassium / fiber / saturated-fat targets

- Blue Zones publishes no formal numeric sodium, potassium, or saturated-fat target table (unlike DASH). Its published guidance is servings/portion-based (beans, nuts, grains) rather than nutrient-gram-based.
- Directionally, the heavy legume/vegetable/whole-grain base implies high fiber and potassium and low sodium/saturated fat by construction, but this is inferred from food rules rather than stated as an explicit numeric target in Blue Zones' own materials.

## 4. Portfolio Diet

### Evidence with effect sizes

- **Original trial** (Jenkins et al., 2003, *JAMA*, "Effects of a Dietary Portfolio of Cholesterol-Lowering Foods vs a Statin Medication on Serum Lipids and C-Reactive Protein" [title paraphrased here to avoid naming a specific drug; see reference list for the exact published title via DOI link]): a short (~1 month) controlled trial in 46 hyperlipidemic adults comparing (a) a low-saturated-fat control diet, (b) the same diet plus a statin, and (c) the "Portfolio" diet (viscous fiber + plant sterols + soy protein + nuts). The Portfolio diet reduced LDL-C by roughly **30%**, statistically similar to the **~31%** reduction from the statin arm, versus only about **8%** for the low-saturated-fat control diet alone. [JAMA 2003](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/196970)
- **Pooled meta-analysis** (Chiavaroli et al., 2018, *Progress in Cardiovascular Diseases*, "Portfolio Dietary Pattern and Cardiovascular Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis of Controlled Trials"; searched MEDLINE/EMBASE/Cochrane through April 2018): across 7 trial comparisons (439 participants with elevated cholesterol), the Portfolio pattern reduced LDL-C by a pooled **~17%** (mean difference −0.73 mmol/L, 95% CI −0.89 to −0.56), with high-certainty evidence for the LDL and most other lipid outcomes (non-HDL-C, apoB, total cholesterol, triglycerides). The same analysis reported improvements in systolic/diastolic blood pressure, CRP, and estimated 10-year coronary heart disease risk as secondary outcomes, and found **no significant effect on HDL-C or body weight**. [PubMed abstract](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29807048/) — [ScienceDirect record](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S003306201830094X)
- Real-world adherence caveat: reviews note that outside tightly controlled feeding trials, self-prepared/free-living adherence typically yields **more modest (~5–15%)** reductions than the ~17–30%+ seen in controlled trials. [News-Medical: Portfolio Diet evidence overview](https://www.news-medical.net/health/Portfolio-Diet-Evidence-Based-Steps-to-Reduce-LDL-Cholesterol.aspx)

### Core food rules (practical terms)

The Portfolio diet is built by layering four specific cholesterol-lowering food categories onto a baseline low-saturated-fat diet:

- **Viscous/soluble fiber:** oats, barley, psyllium, okra, eggplant.
- **Plant sterols/stanols:** typically from fortified foods (sterol-enriched margarine, orange juice, etc.) since naturally-occurring amounts are too low to hit target intake.
- **Soy protein:** soy milk, tofu, soy-based meat alternatives.
- **Tree nuts:** almonds, walnuts, etc.
- **Eat less:** saturated fat and cholesterol-rich foods generally (this diet is explicitly a fortification of a low-sat-fat baseline, not a standalone replacement for one).

[Portfolio Diet official site — components & targets](https://portfoliodiet.org/) — [Cleveland Clinic: The What and How of the Portfolio Diet](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/portfolio-diet)

### Sodium / potassium / fiber / saturated-fat targets (the diet's own published component targets)

- **Viscous fiber:** ~**20 g/day**.
- **Plant sterols/stanols:** ~**2 g/day** (typically requires fortified foods to reach).
- **Soy/plant protein:** ~**50 g/day**.
- **Nuts:** ~**45 g/day** (roughly a small handful).
- No specific sodium or potassium target is published as part of the Portfolio diet itself — it is a cholesterol-focused overlay, not a blood-pressure-focused pattern.

[Portfolio Diet official site — components & targets](https://portfoliodiet.org/)

## 5. Practicality Assessment for an Endurance Athlete on Drug-Managed LDL

**Framing:** because LDL is already being managed pharmacologically, the diet selection here should be weighted toward its **blood pressure** and **weight/satiety** properties, not its LDL-lowering potency. A diet that is excellent for LDL but hard to sustain at high training volume (Portfolio) is a worse fit than one that's merely "good enough" on LDL but easy to sustain and good for BP and weight (DASH, Mediterranean).

- **Caloric/carbohydrate needs vs. diet constraints:** high-volume cycling/running training requires substantial carbohydrate intake and flexible total calories, especially around long or hard sessions. DASH and Mediterranean both center on whole grains, fruit, legumes, and (for Mediterranean) nuts/olive oil — all compatible with high carbohydrate and calorie needs; neither is calorically restrictive by design. The Portfolio diet's fortified/functional-food components (sterol-fortified products, soy protein) can be calorie-light and are more oriented toward LDL-active compounds than bulk energy, making it a less natural fit for fueling volume. Blue Zones' legume-heavy, plant-slant pattern is calorically compatible but its traditionally low meat/protein-density approach may need active protein supplementation (soy, legumes, fish) to meet endurance-athlete protein needs.
- **Sodium tension:** this is the most athlete-specific friction point. DASH's headline BP benefit is partly sodium-driven (the DASH-Sodium trial's largest effect came from *combining* the DASH food pattern with a 1,500 mg/day sodium ceiling), but a sweaty endurance athlete training in volume can have materially elevated sodium losses and needs, particularly in heat or on long/hot rides and runs. A strict 1,500–2,300 mg/day ceiling designed for a sedentary hypertensive population may not be appropriate, or may need in-workout electrolyte fueling carved out as an explicit exception, rather than applying the DASH sodium ceiling to total daily intake including training fuel. Mediterranean, by contrast, does not impose a hard sodium ceiling, which removes this tension but also removes some of DASH's most potent BP-lowering lever.
- **Grocery/meal-prep practicality:**
  - **DASH** — easiest of the four to shop and cook for; ordinary supermarket staples, no fortified/specialty products required, works well for batch cooking.
  - **Mediterranean** — similarly easy, arguably the most "restaurant-and-travel-friendly" of the four given how common Mediterranean-style options are; olive oil, produce, legumes, and fish are all mainstream grocery items.
  - **Blue Zones** — moderately easy (beans, whole grains, vegetables are cheap and simple), but the traditional pattern's very low meat/dairy/egg allowances may require more intentional protein planning for an athlete, and some of its specific regional foods aren't practical substitutes (this is a pattern to borrow principles from, not follow literally).
  - **Portfolio** — the most demanding: it requires specific functional ingredients (sterol-fortified foods, a daily viscous-fiber target, soy protein, a set nuts allotment) tracked against numeric daily targets, which is a meaningfully higher prep/tracking burden than the other three.
- **Where a diet does double duty:** Mediterranean is the strongest "two birds" option here — it has trial-level evidence for both BP reduction (Cochrane pooled data) and cardiovascular event/LDL benefit (PREDIMED, Cochrane), while remaining calorically permissive and low-friction to shop/cook for. DASH does double duty as well (BP is its primary evidence base, with LDL as a secondary/incidental benefit). Portfolio is essentially single-purpose (LDL) and is the pattern with the least marginal value here given the LDL side is already pharmacologically covered.

## 6. Recommended Starting Approach

**Per the cardiologist's actual instruction, DASH is the recommended starting point** — this is a restatement of the physician's directive, not an independent clinical recommendation from this memo. DASH has the strongest, most directly-relevant RCT evidence base for blood pressure reduction specifically (the outcome that matters most here), it's the easiest of the four patterns to shop and meal-prep for, and it's compatible with high training-volume eating once the sodium target is adapted to account for training-related sodium losses (e.g., not counting in-workout electrolyte fueling against the daily ceiling, and reassessing the 2,300 vs. 1,500 mg target with the prescribing physician in light of training sweat losses).

**Blue Zones as a secondary/complementary layer:** rather than adopting Blue Zones as a standalone pattern (its evidence base is observational and, per recent critical reviews, has real data-quality concerns), it's reasonable to layer specific Blue Zones principles on top of a DASH foundation once DASH is established — particularly its legume-forward eating (DASH already recommends beans/legumes but Blue Zones pushes them further, ~daily rather than weekly), its emphasis on minimally processed whole foods, and its eating-pattern practices (eating until moderately full, social/unhurried meals). This is additive rather than a replacement.

**Where Mediterranean and Portfolio fit:**
- **Mediterranean** is highly compatible with DASH — the two patterns overlap substantially (produce, whole grains, legumes, lean protein, minimal processed food) and Mediterranean could reasonably be evaluated as a close second option or even a natural evolution of a DASH-based approach, especially given its additional cardiovascular-event-level evidence (PREDIMED) and its lack of a hard sodium ceiling, which may suit training sodium needs better.
- **Portfolio** is a more specialized, LDL-focused add-on. Given LDL is already pharmacologically managed, Portfolio's specific, tracking-intensive components (fortified sterols, viscous fiber target, soy protein target) are lower priority — it could be considered later as a targeted layer only if LDL management goals change, but it's not where the near-term dietary effort should go.

## 7. AHA Physical Activity Guidelines (Adults)

Per the American Heart Association's current adult physical activity recommendations:

- **Aerobic activity:** at least **150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity** aerobic activity, **or 75 minutes/week of vigorous-intensity** aerobic activity, or an equivalent combination of both — preferably spread across the week.
- **Additional benefit threshold:** even greater benefit is gained by reaching **300 minutes/week** (5 hours) of moderate-intensity activity (or the vigorous equivalent).
- **Strength training:** moderate- to high-intensity **muscle-strengthening activity on at least 2 days per week**.
- **Sedentary behavior:** reduce total sitting time; even light-intensity activity can offset some of the risk associated with prolonged sedentary time.

[AHA: American Heart Association Recommendations for Physical Activity in Adults](https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/exercise-and-physical-activity/fitness-basics/aha-recs-for-physical-activity-in-adults) — [AHA infographic (PDF)](https://www.heart.org/-/media/Healthy-Living-Files/Fitness/AHA_Adult_Physical_Activity_Rec_Infographic_English.pdf)
